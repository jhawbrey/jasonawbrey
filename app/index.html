Bio

Jason has over 14 years of experience in Information Technology with a focus on internet technologies. As a web developer, Jason has worked on a number of brands in numerous industries, including American Airlines, Chemistry.com, Cigna, the Dallas Mavericks, the Dallas Symphony Orchestra, FastSigns, FileMaker, FilesAnywhere, GoRVing.com, Landmark Theaters, Match.com, Michael Kors, Michaels, Patrón, Salvation Army, Thomasville, and Travelocity. Recent projects include a social eCommerce Facebook application and website for a Mark Cuban funded incubator/startup, several interactive television applications for the Yahoo Connected TV platform, and a Facebook dating application for Match.com.

His experience spans from database development through user interface development, but his true passion lies in exploring the possibilities of client side application development using JavaScript to build engaging user experiences on the web.

Bio

Jason Awbrey, baritone, has appeared with such performing organizations as the Dallas Bach Society, Ars Lyrica of Houston, Ft Worth Early Music, Orpheus Chamber Singers, Vox Humana, Orchestra of New Spain, Santa Fe Desert Chorale, Allen Philharmonic, Dallas Symphony, Northeast Symphony and the San Antonio Symphony. His lyric voice has gained him solos in Handel’s Messiah, Haydn's Creation, Montiverdi’s Vespers, Carrisimi’s Jephte, Barber’s Dover Beach, Brahms’ Ein Deutsches Requiem, Fauré’s Requiem, Duruflé’s Requiem and Messe ‘Cum Jubilo’, Howells’ Requiem, the Bach Passions, numerous Bach cantatas, Bach’s Weihnachts-Oratorium, and Vaughan Williams’ Fantasia on Christmas Carols.

Heralded as having “one of the most gorgeous baritones on earth” by the Dallas Morning News, Jason has garnered much critical acclaim for his interpretations of music from the 17th through 21st century. After a recent recital of German lieder, The Dallas Morning News wrote "everything sounded so easy, so natural" and "[it] was sheer magic." In a performance of Schütz’s Die sieben Worte Jesu Christi am Kreuz, Scott Cantrell of the Dallas Morning News said Mr. Awbrey sang “with heartrending beauty and sensitivity.” The Fort Worth Star-Telegram wrote “Baritone Jason Awbrey was a resonant and commanding soloist” in a performance of Copland’s Old American Songs.

Mr. Awbrey can be heard on a number of recordings with the Church of the Incarnation choir, Orpheus Chamber Singers, and a recently released recording on the Naxos label with Vox Humana. Those recordings include a CD of music by Orlando Gibbons, the masses of Howells, Kodály, and Langlais, Duruflé’s Requiem with Jason as the baritone soloist, and a Magnificat and Nunc Dimittis setting written by Joel Martinson dedicated to Mr. Awbrey and his wife Monica.

Classical music review: German lieder elegantly sung by Jason Awbrey

Scott Cantrell, Dallas Morning News Classical Music Critic

Once in a great while there’s a singing voice so beautiful that we can’t quite imagine it’s coming out of a merely human throat. Jason Awbrey has a baritone like that.

Having admired his brief solos in groups like the Orpheus Chamber Singers, Dallas Bach Society and a church choir, I’ve long hoped he would do a song recital someday. Wishes were answered Sunday afternoon, at Zion Lutheran Church, thanks to the art-song series Voces Intimae. The program was billed as a tribute to the late German baritone Dietrich Fischer-Dieskau, whose innumerable recordings did so much to popularize German art songs, or lieder. Awbrey’s program featured repertory associated with his distinguished forebear: three Schubert songs and two song cycles, Beethoven’s An die ferne Geliebte and Schumann’s Dichterliebe.

From the first notes of Schubert’s “An Sylvia,” there was that sound: warmly rich below, rising to top notes either clarion or sweetly floated, as appropriate, everything finely focused. It soon struck me how much it resembled Fischer-Dieskau’s sound, but it may have been even more beautiful. The shift of tone at the words translated as “And the last ray dies/Behind that hilltop,” in the last of the Beethoven songs, was sheer magic.

Apart from a couple of forced high notes, and a few bottom notes wanting more oomph, everything sounded so easy, so natural, qualities most singers can only covet.

Few singers caress words so lovingly as Awbrey. With excellent German diction, although some consonants wanted more projection, he made texts as beautiful as melodies. As a friend said afterward, surely Fischer-Dieskau was smiling somewhere.

Awbrey flawlessly negotiated Schumann’s “Die Rose, die Lilie, die Taube, die Sonne” at quite a brisk speed, but “Ich will meine Seele” and the final “Die alten, bösen Lieder” felt a little hurried.

Brian Bentley accompanied competently most of the time, on a merely serviceable baby grand piano. But he was audibly stressed at some points in the Beethoven and he lagged behind Awbrey in a few places here and elsewhere. Those wonderful curling piano lines in the first of the Dichterliebe were dead on the vine.

The program book was a model of such things, with clearly printed texts and translations. But Voces Intimae representatives added to the ongoing plague of unnecessary blather at concerts. Really, folks, we don’t need to be told that the performers we’re about to hear, or have just heard, are great. But let’s hope they book Awbrey for another recital.
